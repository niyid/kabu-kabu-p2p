Every time you open Uber or Bolt, your precise location is transmitted to commercial servers, stored indefinitely, and potentially shared with governments, advertisers, or data brokers. The open-source Kabu-Kabu app rethinks ride-hailing from the ground up — using I2P onion routing, GeoHash cell anonymisation, and Monero multisig escrow to deliver rides without a trace.

The Problem

Your Location Is a Commodity — and You're Giving It Away for Free

When you request a ride on a conventional platform, a cascade of surveillance begins before the driver even sees your request. Your precise GPS coordinates — accurate to within a few metres — are transmitted to remote servers owned by a private corporation. From there, they may be logged, analysed, sold to data brokers, or handed to law enforcement on receipt of a subpoena that you will never know was issued.

For most users this may feel like an acceptable trade-off for convenience. For activists, journalists, domestic abuse survivors, political dissidents, or simply anyone who values their freedom of movement as a private matter, this trade-off is unacceptable — and in some contexts, genuinely dangerous.

The diagram above illustrates the data flow in any centralised ride-hailing app. A single corporate server acts as the hub of everything: it knows who you are, where you started, where you went, and when — and it can share that information with dozens of third parties, voluntarily or under legal compulsion. Kabu-Kabu was built specifically to eliminate this flow.

The Solution

Kabu-Kabu: A Serverless, Privacy-Native Ride Network

Named after the informal shared-taxi culture prominent in West African cities, Kabu-Kabu is an open-source Android application that connects riders and drivers directly over the I2P anonymity network, with no central server, no corporate data store, and no identity-linked accounts. It was designed from first principles with privacy as a hard requirement — not a marketing feature.

The application is written entirely in Kotlin and targets modern Android. Technical Deep Dive

Four Pillars of Privacy, Engineered into Every Layer

🌐
Pillar 01

I2P Network Transport

All peer-to-peer traffic is routed through an embedded I2P router using 3-hop garlic routing. Neither origin nor destination IP address is visible to any intermediate node. Devices are identified by opaque 512-byte cryptographic destinations — not IP addresses.

📍
Pillar 02

GeoHash Location Anonymisation

Raw GPS coordinates are immediately converted to a GeoHash-5 cell (~5 km × 5 km) and discarded. The precise coordinate is never stored, logged, or transmitted. Trip tracking uses ~5 km zones — enough to follow progress, too coarse for surveillance.

🪙
Pillar 03

Monero Multisig Payments

Fares are settled via Monero 2-of-2 multisig escrow using Monerujo JNI bindings. Transactions are untraceable and unlinkable by design. No bank details, no card numbers, no KYC — payment flows through the same I2P tunnel as all other messages.

🔐
Pillar 04

Pseudonymous Identities

Phone numbers are SHA-256-hashed on first launch. The plaintext number is never stored to disk or transmitted. Drivers and riders know each other only by their I2P destination address — a cryptographic identifier with no binding to real-world identity.

I2P Anonymity Network

Routing Rides Through the Invisible Internet

The Internet Invisible Project (I2P) is an anonymity network that routes traffic through a series of encrypted tunnels across volunteer-operated nodes. Unlike Tor, which uses a centralised directory, I2P uses a fully distributed network database (NetDB). Each message is wrapped in multiple layers of encryption — a technique called garlic routing — before traversing at least three independent hops.

Kabu-Kabu embeds the I2P router directly inside the Android app as a foreground service (I2PKabuService). When the app launches, it bootstraps a local I2P node, contacts reseed servers to populate its peer table, and starts a SAM (Simple Anonymous Messaging) bridge on 127.0.0.1:7656. All ride communications — request broadcasts, driver offers, trip events, and payment messages — then flow exclusively through this local I2P stack.

I2P Garlic Routing — Kabu-Kabu Message Path

The bootstrapping sequence is carefully engineered for Android's constraints. The app copies I2P's own CA certificate bundle from assets/i2p/certificates/ and exports the Android system CA store as PEM files — a critical step since I2P's raw SSL socket bypasses Android's usual certificate handling. Only after SAM bridge readiness is confirmed via a live HELLO VERSION handshake does the app allow any ride-related traffic.

ℹ️
Startup latency note

On first boot, the I2P router must reseed by contacting bootstrap servers to build its peer table. This takes 2–5 minutes but is a one-time cost. Subsequent launches reuse the cached netDb and are significantly faster. The app clearly signals I2P bootstrap state in the UI via MSG_TYPE_I2P_STATE messages.

Location Privacy

Where Precisely? Nowhere. Where Approximately? Zone EJ3F.

GeoHash is a hierarchical spatial data structure that divides the Earth's surface into a grid of rectangular cells, each identified by a short alphanumeric string. The precision of the hash string determines the cell size: more characters means a smaller, more precise area.

GeoHash Precision Levels Used in Kabu-Kabu

Kabu-Kabu enforces a strict rule in GeoHashPrivacyUtil: the function toMatchCell(lat, lon) is the only place in the entire codebase that is permitted to receive raw GPS coordinates. Immediately after computing the GeoHash-5 string, the raw latitude and longitude values are discarded. They never touch the database, the network, or any log file.

GeoHashPrivacyUtil.kt — Core Privacy Invariant Kotlin
/** * Convert raw GPS coordinates to a GeoHash-5 string. * This is the ONLY function that should ever receive raw lat/lng * from the GPS sensor. After this call, discard the raw values. */ fun toMatchCell(latitude: Double, longitude: Double): String = GeoHash.withCharacterPrecision(latitude, longitude, MATCH_PRECISION).toBase32() // During an active trip, the driver's position is broadcast as: data class TripEvent( val tripId: String, val driverId: String, val eventType: TripEventType, val currentGeohash: String, // ← ~5km cell, NEVER exact GPS val messageText: String = "", val timestamp: Long )

Even during an active trip, when the driver is periodically broadcasting their position so the rider can track progress, only the GeoHash-5 cell is sent — never the driver's precise GPS fix. This design gives riders enough information to know when their driver is approaching or has arrived, while preventing the creation of a high-resolution location trail that could later be used to reconstruct anyone's movements.

End-to-End Flow

A Complete Ride — From Request to Payment, Zero Data Leaked

Kabu-Kabu Ride Lifecycle — Privacy-Annotated
01
Rider requests a ride

GPS is read and immediately hashed to a GeoHash-5 cell. A RideRequest object is created containing only: geohash pickup/dropoff zones, service type (Taxi/Courier), a SHA-256-derived rider ID, and an on-device fare estimate in XMR millicents. No name, address, or precise coordinate is included.

✓ No raw GPS transmitted ✓ No identity revealed
02
Request broadcast over I2P

The I2PKabuClient sends the ride request to the local I2PKabuService via a TCP socket on 127.0.0.1:8881. The service broadcasts the request to all peers in the rider's GeoHash-5 cell and its 8 neighbours — a ~20 km discovery radius — through encrypted I2P tunnels. No server receives or routes the broadcast.

✓ Garlic-routed · 3+ hops ✓ No central broker
03
Drivers respond with offers

Drivers in adjacent cells receive the broadcast and may respond with a DriverOffer containing: their I2P destination address (a 512-byte cryptographic blob), vehicle type, ETA in minutes, and an optional counter-fare. No phone number, name, or GPS coordinate is included in the offer.

✓ Driver identity = I2P destination only
04
Direct encrypted tunnel established

Once the rider accepts an offer, a dedicated end-to-end encrypted I2P tunnel is opened directly between the two devices. All subsequent communication — trip events, location updates, wallet messages — travels exclusively through this tunnel. No third party can intercept or inspect the payload.

✓ End-to-end encrypted ✓ Peer-to-peer direct
05
XMR multisig escrow funded

The RideWalletManager orchestrates a Monero 2-of-2 multisig escrow. Both devices exchange multisig wallet information over the I2P tunnel. The rider funds the escrow address with the agreed XMR fare before the trip begins. The escrow can only be released when both rider and driver co-sign the transaction — eliminating payment fraud in both directions.

✓ XMR untraceable by design ✓ No custodian
06
Trip in progress — zone-level tracking only

As the driver moves, periodic TripEvent messages are sent over the direct tunnel, each carrying the driver's current GeoHash-5 cell (~5 km precision). This gives the rider a live sense of progress without creating a sub-metre GPS trail of the driver's route.

✓ ~5 km accuracy · no sub-metre trail
07
Trip completes — escrow released, trip logged locally

On TRIP_COMPLETED, the rider exports a partial transaction signature and sends it to the driver, who co-signs and broadcasts the Monero transaction. Trip records are written to the on-device Room database only — never synced to any remote server. No third party ever learns that this ride occurred.

✓ Payment broadcast · escrow closed ✓ History stored locally only
Architecture

Why Serverless P2P Changes Everything

The most fundamental difference between Kabu-Kabu and conventional ride-hailing is not any individual privacy feature — it is the elimination of the central server entirely. The diagram below, included in the Kabu-Kabu project repository, illustrates this architectural contrast and its consequences.

In a hub-and-spoke client-server topology (left panel), every message must pass through the central authority. The server can read, log, analyse, or share any of it. Payments

Invisible Money for Invisible Rides

The payment system is the final piece of the privacy puzzle. Even if every network hop were perfectly anonymous, a payment processed through a conventional payment rail would create a linkable record: "On Tuesday at 14:23, user X paid for a 7-minute trip."

Kabu-Kabu uses Monero (XMR), a privacy-focused cryptocurrency that employs stealth addresses, ring signatures, and RingCT to make transactions untraceable and unlinkable by default. The fare estimation itself is handled entirely on-device by FareEstimator.kt, using a locally-stored rate table and the Haversine formula applied to the GeoHash cell centres — so not even a pricing server learns where you are going.

FareEstimator.kt — On-Device, Zero-Server Pricing Kotlin
/** * Privacy: all inputs and outputs stay on-device. * No pricing server is contacted. The fare estimate carried in * RideRequest.fareEstimateXMR is computed here before being broadcast. */ fun estimate( pickupGeohash: String, dropoffGeohash: String, serviceType: ServiceType, hourOfDayWAT: Int = currentHourWAT() ): Long { val distanceKm = haversineKm(pickupGeohash, dropoffGeohash) val isPeak = hourOfDayWAT in PEAK_HOURS // 07–09, 16–20 WAT val raw = BASE_FARE_XMR + (distanceKm * ratePerKm).toLong() return (if (isPeak) raw * PEAK_MULTIPLIER else raw) .coerceAtLeast(MINIMUM_FARE_XMR) }

The 2-of-2 multisig escrow model is particularly elegant: neither party can unilaterally claim the funds. The rider's money is locked until both co-sign. If either party disappears, the escrow can be resolved on-chain without any arbitration service — no Kabu-Kabu operator ever touches the money.

🛡️
Zero-custodian payment guarantee

Because Kabu-Kabu has no central operator and all payments flow through on-device Monero wallets via Monerujo JNI bindings, there is no company account to freeze, no payment processor to involve, and no financial record linkable to your identity anywhere in the system.

Honest Assessment

Real Trade-offs and Remaining Challenges

Privacy is never free. Kabu-Kabu makes principled trade-offs that users should understand before relying on it.

⚡
Latency: the price of anonymity

I2P's multi-hop garlic routing introduces additional latency compared to direct connections. First-boot I2P initialisation can take 2–5 minutes. Message delivery times of a few extra seconds are expected. For a ride-hailing context this is generally acceptable; for real-time navigation it is not a replacement for dedicated mapping tools.

📴
Both peers must be online

Unlike centralised apps where a server buffers messages for offline users, P2P communication requires both the rider and driver to be online simultaneously. This is mitigated by the 10-minute TTL on RideRequests, but it means a driver who loses connectivity mid-trip cannot silently reconnect without the rider noticing.

🔧
Network effects and adoption

A P2P network's utility depends on the number of active peers. In a city with few Kabu-Kabu users, wait times will be long and driver availability limited. The app is ideally suited to privacy-conscious communities, activist networks, or regions where surveillance of movement carries genuine risk — rather than as a mass-market replacement for mainstream apps.

Conclusion

The Future of Mobility Is Private — or It Is Nothing

Kabu-Kabu demonstrates something that many considered impossible: a fully functional, privacy-preserving ride-hailing network with no central server, no corporate data collection, and no payment trail. By combining I2P onion routing, GeoHash spatial anonymisation, Monero multisig escrow, and SHA-256 pseudonymous identities, it delivers an experience that genuinely protects its users.

The project is not without challenges. Bootstrap latency, the requirement for simultaneous online presence, and the cold-start network-effects problem are real. But for the communities for whom location privacy is a matter of safety — not preference — these are acceptable costs. The alternative, broadcasting precise GPS coordinates to corporate servers in real time, is simply not an option.

Privacy in movement is not a luxury. Knowing where you are, where you have been, and where you are going is among the most sensitive data about a human life. Kabu-Kabu treats it accordingly.
