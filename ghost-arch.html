The Problem

The Problem With "Privacy" as a Marketing Label

Most apps that claim to be privacy-respecting still route your data through a central server owned by a company, in a jurisdiction with its own subpoena regime and data-retention laws. Even end-to-end encrypted messengers typically rely on central infrastructure to broker connections, store metadata, and manage keys. When that server is compelled, hacked, or sold, your privacy disappears.

A quieter movement has been building a different answer: what if the server simply did not exist?

This article explores a concrete engineering paradigm for building genuinely serverless, privacy-by-default mobile apps — one pioneered by a small developer studio based in Lagos, Nigeria, using two open-source primitives that have been battle-tested by the privacy community: the I2P anonymity network and Monero (XMR) privacy cryptocurrency. We use Kabu-Kabu, an open-source P2P ride-hailing app, as the primary technical case study, and survey three other apps already live on Google Play that implement the same paradigm.

Foundations

The Two Primitives

🌐
Primitive 01

I2P — The Invisible Internet Project

A fully encrypted P2P overlay network resistant to traffic analysis. Its garlic routing bundles multiple encrypted messages together, making sender/receiver correlation far harder than Tor's onion routing. Critically, I2P can be embedded directly into an Android app — no separate install, no VPN profile, no permission gymnastics.

🪙
Primitive 02

Monero — Privacy by Protocol

Every transaction uses ring signatures, stealth addresses, and RingCT to hide sender, recipient, and amount by default — not as an option. Monero's multisig wallets let funds be locked under joint control and released only by threshold consensus, enabling trustless escrow, savings circles, and peer betting with no central custodian.

When the user opens an app built on I2P, a fully functional I2P node starts on-device, connects to the network over the standard internet connection, and all subsequent peer communication is anonymised automatically. The app is the router.

The Paradigm

Five Design Principles

The apps covered in this article share a coherent set of design decisions that together constitute a replicable paradigm for privacy-first mobile development.

1
No central server

All communication happens between devices over I2P. There is no relay server, no cloud database, no API endpoint belonging to the developer. The developer cannot be compelled to hand over user data because they hold none.

2
On-device identity through one-way hashing

User identifiers — typically a phone number or device ID — are SHA-256 hashed on-device before ever being used as a network identifier. The plaintext never leaves the phone.

3
Fuzzy location, not precise GPS

Where location is needed, raw GPS coordinates are immediately converted to a GeoHash-5 cell (~4.9 km × 4.9 km). The raw latitude/longitude is discarded and never transmitted.

4
Local-only persistence

Trip records, incident reports, skill listings, and trade states are stored in on-device databases (Room / SQLite). No cloud backup — enforced at the manifest level via backup_rules.xml and data_extraction_rules.xml.

5
Monero for financial flows

Where the app handles money, Monero's multisig capability ensures the developer never holds user funds, and transactions are private by protocol rather than by policy.

Case Study

Kabu-Kabu — Privacy-First P2P Ride-Hailing

Kabu-Kabu is an open-source Android app for peer-to-peer taxi and courier matching, explicitly designed to replace centralised ride-hailing platforms like Uber or Bolt with a serverless alternative.

Every major ride-hailing platform is architecturally a surveillance system. Exact GPS coordinates are continuously uploaded to a central server. Your real phone number is stored and linked to every trip. The company can reconstruct your location history, travel patterns, and social graph from this data — and third-party data brokers often receive a share of it.

Technical architecture

Kabu-Kabu — Device-Local Architecture Overview
Rider/Driver device ├── MainActivity / DriverActivity │ └── I2PKabuClient ──TCP(127.0.0.1:8881)──▶ │ I2PKabuService (foreground) │ ├── EmbeddedI2PRouter │ ├── Local TCP bridge │ └── KabuDatabase (Room) │ └── Privacy layer: GeoHash-5 only (no raw GPS over network) SHA-256 device identity (no plaintext phone) I2P garlic routing (no IP exposure) No cloud backup

The EmbeddedI2PRouter manages the full I2P router lifecycle as a foreground Android service. The I2PKabuClient connects to it via a local TCP socket, making the I2P layer invisible to the application logic above it. Ride requests are broadcast to peers within the same GeoHash cell; once a driver accepts, a direct I2P tunnel is established for the trip itself.

Why this matters for ride-hailing specifically

Ride-hailing data is among the most sensitive location data that exists — it records where you live, work, worship, who you visit, and when. In jurisdictions with authoritarian governance, this data can be used against users. Kabu-Kabu's architecture means this data simply does not exist outside the user's own device.

The Ecosystem

Three Apps Already Live on Google Play

Kabu-Kabu is the newest member of an already-deployed ecosystem of apps by Techducat Team that implement the same I2P/Monero paradigm across five distinct use cases.

Once the I2P substrate is built and tested, new privacy-preserving applications can be built on top of it with relatively modest additional effort.

— On the reusability of the paradigm

Buzzr — P2P Community Safety Alerts

Buzzr turns each phone into a community safety node. When something happens nearby — an accident, fire, robbery, or flood — a user reports it instantly. The incident broadcasts to nearby peers over I2P, categorised (FIRE · ACCIDENT · ROBBERY · FLOOD · FIGHT), and stored locally for 48 hours. No account required, zero data collection per the Play Store safety declaration, and it has crossed 1,000+ installs — the most-deployed app in the ecosystem.

6°Net — Privacy-First P2P Skill Networking

6°Net is a peer-to-peer professional network inspired by six degrees of separation. Users search for skills across 2, 5, or 8 hops in their existing contact graph. Your contact list is SHA-256 hashed entirely on-device — only anonymised peer IDs ever leave the phone. A recent update removed Google Ads, Firebase, and all relay-server dependencies, transitioning to a fully serverless architecture.

ScrowIt! — XMR Escrow

ScrowIt! is where the Monero half of the paradigm comes fully into view — a non-custodial P2P escrow app using 2-of-3 multisig: funds locked jointly between Buyer, Seller, and a neutral Arbitrator. No single party can move funds alone. A fee of 1.5% is collected atomically at payout, and offline counterparties are handled via an end-to-end encrypted Firestore mailbox as a store-and-forward relay.

Àjọ — Monero Savings Circle

Àjọ digitises the traditional rotating savings circle (tontine, chit fund, esusu) with no accounts of any kind — identity lives only as a cryptographic key on-device. It generalises multisig to (n-1)-of-n across 3–20 members, so no single member, including the creator, can move funds unilaterally. Payouts can follow a fixed sequence, a verifiable random lottery, or a bidding mechanism.

Versuz — P2P Skill Betting

Versuz applies the same non-custodial mechanism to peer-to-peer skill challenges: Challenger and Opponent lock stakes into a 2-of-3 multisig with a neutral Arbitrator as third key holder — no bookie, no house, beyond a transparent 1.5% fee. Rated 18+ on Google Play, it carries an explicit disclaimer that Monero transactions are irreversible.

Implementation Guide

For Developers: How to Replicate This Paradigm

The Kabu-Kabu codebase is open source and designed to be forked. The core steps for building a new app on this substrate:

1
Embed the I2P router

Obtain router.jar, sam.jar, and i2p-android-client.aar, place them in app/libs/. The SAM bridge exposes a plain TCP socket API so app code never touches I2P internals directly.

2
Define your GeoHash zone

Convert GPS coordinates to a GeoHash-5 string immediately on receipt (e.g. via ch.hsr:geohash). Use it as the "zone" for peer discovery. Discard raw coordinates.

3
Hash your identity

Hash the user's phone number or persistent device identifier with SHA-256 on first launch. Store the hash, discard the plaintext, and use the hash as your node's identity.

4
Define your domain messages

Kabu-Kabu uses RideRequest/DriverOffer; Buzzr uses IncidentReport; 6°Net uses SkillListing/SearchQuery. Serialise your domain objects to JSON and broadcast over the I2P socket.

5
Disable cloud backup

Add android:allowBackup="false" to your manifest and configure backup_rules.xml to exclude your Room database.

6
Add Monero if money is involved

Integrate the Monero Android wallet library and use 2-of-3 multisig for any escrow or joint-custody scenario, keeping the app non-custodial by design.

Build flavours for playstore and fdroid are recommended from the start — F-Droid users are exactly the audience this paradigm serves, and a reproducible build demonstrates that the binary matches the published source.

Conclusion

The Developer Cannot Betray Their Users' Data

The apps described in this article — Kabu-Kabu, Buzzr, 6°Net, ScrowIt!, Àjọ, and Versuz — represent something genuinely new in the Android ecosystem: a coherent, replicable paradigm for building applications where privacy is an architectural property rather than a policy claim.

The paradigm is not theoretical. It is running on Google Play today, serving real users, with open-source code available for inspection. It does not require exotic hardware, custom operating systems, or deep cryptographic expertise. It requires two mature open-source primitives, a handful of well-understood Android libraries, and the discipline to never write user data to a server you control.

The most remarkable thing about this approach is its simplicity as a trust model: the developer cannot betray their users' data because they never had it.

— Article summary

That is the paradigm. The code is available. The question is how many more apps will be built on it.
